## S4: METHOD CHANGE PROPOSAL

**PROPOSED CHANGE:** Implement a graduated IP-sample penalty for L14 xFIP/SIERA metrics that scales the weight assigned to the recent metric inversely with the IP count. Specifically: L14 IP ≥ 35 → full weight; 20-34 IP → 70% weight, blend 30% toward AGG; 11-19 IP → 40% weight, blend 60% toward AGG; <11 IP → treat as anecdotal only, lead with AGG.

**WHAT IN MY METHOD COULD BE SHARPER:** I currently flag small-sample L14 windows in my written reasoning (I noted Ashcraft's 11 IP explicitly, Miller's small sample) but I do not systematically downgrade those metrics in my probability estimate. The verbal flag exists; the numerical adjustment does not. The result is that I write the caveat and then don't fully act on it — I still assigned Ashcraft a significant ace-level edge and estimated PIT's win probability at 64%, which is too high once the L14 xFIP is properly discounted to ~70% AGG weighting.

**EVIDENCE FROM TODAY (pre-game data only):** Ashcraft L14 xFIP 2.05 on 11 IP — flagged as small sample in reasoning but probability estimate of 64% PIT win still implicitly relied on the 2.05 figure heavily. Bryce Miller — correctly handled with 1-unit cap, but the same IP-scaling logic should have been applied more formally. Sasaki's recent elite metrics similarly rested on limited IP in the L14 window. Three separate games where I verbally flagged sample size but didn't translate the flag into a consistent numerical discount.

**CONFIDENCE:** MEDIUM — one slate is insufficient to confirm, but the pattern of "flag it verbally, ignore it numerically" appeared in at least three separate picks. The graduated penalty would have reduced PIT confidence, potentially made SEA still a bet at the same 1-unit level, and potentially eliminated LAD -152 as a bet (or reduced it to a marginal/pass). The MIL best bet was unaffected (78 IP explicitly noted as non-small-sample). Testing over 20+ slates would confirm whether the graduated discount improves overall calibration.
